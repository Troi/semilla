Structure

Disclaimer: This is a working model, not a legal commitment. Final structure will be developed with Uruguayan lawyer and will reflect needs of specific founders. Numbers and conditions below are illustrative.

Why structure matters

Most intentional communities collapse on bad structure. Either ownership is fragmented (everyone owns piece of land, nobody can leave), or governance is unclear (who decides what), or exit is impossible (you're trapped).

Semilla is designed to solve these problems from the start. Structure must be:

  • Legal – Uruguayan law must recognize it
  • Flexible – rules can change as community grows
  • Fair – everyone knows what they get and give
  • Exitable – you can leave under defined conditions

Uruguayan nominative SA

What it is: SA (Sociedad Anónima) is Uruguayan business corporation, equivalent of Czech a.s. or American Inc. "Nominative" means shares are registered – you know who owns.

Why SA:

  • Standard structure – Uruguayan lawyers know it, it's not experiment
  • Legal entity – protects member-shareholders from personal liability
  • Flexible bylaws – rules can change by member-shareholder vote
  • Tax efficiency – Uruguay has no tax on worldwide income for non-residents

What SA owns:

  • All land
  • All buildings and infrastructure
  • Machines, tools, supplies
  • Animals, plants, production

What member-shareholders own:

  • SA shares proportional to investment
  • Rights defined in bylaws and member contract
  • Nothing else – no pieces of land, no buildings in own name

Member contract beyond SA

SA bylaws define ownership and basic governance. Member contract defines rights and obligations of member-shareholders beyond what SA bylaws cover.

What member contract contains:

Baseline guarantees

What each member-shareholder gets minimum, regardless of what happens:

  • Access to production – share of farm production at cost price (vegetables, fruit, eggs, dairy products). Cost price = actual production costs, not market price.
  • Residency support – SA helps with residency process (lawyer, documents, fees). Residency not guaranteed (depends on Uruguayan state), but SA does maximum.
  • Physical access – right to spend time on farm (vacation, remote work, long-term stay). Limited by housing capacity and reasonableness.
  • Information – transparent reporting on finances, production, decisions. Minimum quarterly report.
  • Exit – right to leave under defined conditions (see below).

Member-shareholder obligations

  • Respect for property – SA property is shared, not your personal. Don't destroy, steal, pollute.
  • Participation in governance – vote in relevant chambers, participate in discussions (if you're builder).
  • Following rules – SA bylaws and member contract are binding.
  • Physical presence – if you're builder, minimum 3 months per year (first 3 years). If you're investor, no obligation.
  • Skill contribution – if you're builder, use your skills for community (paid work or barter).

Exit conditions

Voluntary exit (you want to leave):

  • You can sell shares back to SA or to another member-shareholder
  • Share price = fair market value determined by independent valuation (Uruguayan appraiser)
  • SA has right of first refusal (can buy shares before third party)
  • Payment within 12 months (not immediate – SA needs time to raise capital)
  • If SA can't pay, shares sold to third party (with prior consent of chamber of legitimacy)

Forced exit (community expels you):

  • Possible only for serious rule violations (theft, violence, repeated governance ignoring)
  • Requires 2/3 majority in chamber of legitimacy
  • Share price = fair market value minus damages (if any)
  • Payment within 24 months
  • No farm access after expulsion decision

Exit during SA liquidation:

  • If SA collapses (bankruptcy, community breakdown, force majeure)
  • Property sold, debts paid, remainder distributed among member-shareholders proportional to shares
  • Consumers are last in line (get only if something remains)

Opt-in entities

Some things aren't mandatory for all member-shareholders. They're voluntary cooperatives beyond SA.

Health fund:

  • Voluntary – you can join or not
  • Shared healthcare financing (clinic, doctor, equipment)
  • Contribution proportional to family size
  • Priority access to care for fund members
  • Non-members pay market price

Educational cooperative:

  • Voluntary – only for families with children
  • Shared school financing (teacher, materials, building)
  • Contribution proportional to number of children
  • Decision-making on curriculum, methods, teachers
  • Non-members have no access

Why opt-in:

  • Not everyone wants to pay for school when they have no children
  • Not everyone wants to pay for clinic when they're healthy
  • Voluntariness eliminates conflicts
  • Those who pay decide

Governance – four-chamber system

Details in Pillars section, but here's summary of how it works in practice:

Chamber of legitimacy (1 person = 1 vote):

  • Accepting new members
  • Changes to SA bylaws
  • Changes to member contract
  • Member expulsion (2/3 majority)

Chamber of taxes (voting power = capital contribution):

  • Budget and investments
  • Financial commitments
  • SA property sale
  • Dividends (if SA generates profit)

Chamber of defense (voting power = days on farm in last year):

  • Security measures
  • Conflict situations
  • Crisis management
  • Physical access rules

Chamber of children (voting power = number of children in community):

  • Long-term investments (orchards, buildings)
  • Education
  • Production sustainability
  • Environmental decisions

How decisions are made:

  • Each decision has defined chamber(s) that must approve
  • Budget = chamber of taxes + chamber of legitimacy
  • Security = chamber of defense + chamber of legitimacy
  • Long-term investments = chamber of taxes + chamber of children
  • Majority = 50%+1, fundamental changes = 2/3

Example: How it works in practice

Scenario 1: Investor wants to leave after 3 years

  1. Investor announces intent to leave (in writing, 3 months notice)
  2. SA has shares valued by independent appraiser (Uruguayan valuer)
  3. SA has right of first refusal – can buy shares at appraised price
  4. If SA has no capital, investor can sell to third party (with prior consent of chamber of legitimacy)
  5. Payment within 12 months
  6. Investor leaves, SA continues

Scenario 2: Builder stops fulfilling obligations

  1. Builder stops coming (0 days in last year)
  2. Chamber of defense warns them (in writing, 30 days to remedy)
  3. If nothing changes, chamber of legitimacy votes on expulsion (2/3 majority)
  4. If passes, builder must leave
  5. Shares valued, damages deducted (if any)
  6. Payment within 24 months
  7. Builder leaves, SA continues

Scenario 3: Community wants to build clinic

  1. Someone proposes investment (clinic, $100k)
  2. Chamber of taxes votes (those who pay) – passes 60% votes
  3. Chamber of children votes (future) – passes 70% votes
  4. Chamber of legitimacy votes (everyone agrees) – passes 55% votes
  5. Investment approved, SA builds clinic
  6. Opt-in health fund created for those who want to pay for operation

What can change

This is working model. Final structure will depend on:

  • Uruguayan law – what's legal and what's not
  • Founders – what specific people need
  • Costs – what's financially realistic
  • Risks – what Uruguayan lawyer recommends

But principles remain:

  • Centralized ownership (SA owns everything)
  • Four-chamber governance (balanced power)
  • Defined exit (you can leave)
  • Baseline guarantees (you know what you get)
  • Opt-in entities (voluntariness)
